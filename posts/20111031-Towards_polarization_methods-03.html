Towards polarization methods, part 3: the energy principle of Hashin and Shtrikman

Towards polarization methods, part 3: the energy principle of Hashin and Shtrikman

In the second part of this series, I introduced the variational principle of Hashin and Shtrikman (1962). It should be noted that this principle is valid, regardless of the reference material \(\tens{C}_0\) (no restriction apply to the elastic moduli of the reference material). In the present instalment, we will see under which circumstances this principle is in fact an energy principle. By energy principle, I mean that the functional of Hashin and Shtrikman \(\mathcal H\) (see part 2)

\begin{equation*} \mathcal H(\tens{\tau})=\overline{\tens{\varepsilon}}:\volavg{\tens{\tau}}-\frac12\volavg{\tens{\tau}:\left(\tens{C}-\tens{C}_0\right)^{-1}:\tens{\tau}}-\frac12\volavg{\tens{\tau}:\left(\tens{\Gamma}_0\circledast\tens{\tau}\right)}, \end{equation*}

is extremum at the solution of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation (we already know that it is stationary).

This instalment will in fact be very short, as you will be spared the proof of the main result. As in part 2, all details can be found in Hashin and Shtrikman (1962) [see also Willis (1977) for a slightly more modern proof]. So, here goes.

Let \(\tens{\tau}\) be the unique solution of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation (see part 1)

\begin{equation*} \left(\tens{C}-\tens{C}_0\right)^{-1}:\tens{\tau}+\tens{\Gamma}_0\circledast\tens{\tau}=\overline{\tens{\varepsilon}}. \end{equation*}

If the reference material is stiffer (resp. softer) than the heterogeneous material, then \(\mathcal H\) is minimum (resp. maximum) at \(\tens{\tau}\). More precisely

  • if \(\tens{C}(\vec x)-\tens{C}_0\) is positive, semi-definite at every point \(\vec x\in\Omega\), then
\begin{equation*} \text{for all}\;\tens{\varpi}(\vec x),\mathcal H(\tens{\varpi})\leq\mathcal H(\tens{\tau}), \end{equation*}
  • if \(\tens{C}(\vec x)-\tens{C}_0\) is negative, semi-definite at every point \(\vec x\in\Omega\), then
\begin{equation*} \text{for all}\;\tens{\varpi}(\vec x),\mathcal H(\tens{\varpi})\geq\mathcal H(\tens{\tau}). \end{equation*}

Maybe a quick reminder would be useful, here. \(\tens{C} - \tens{C}_0\) is said to be positive, semi-definite, if \(\tens{\varpi}:\tens{C}:\tens{\varpi}\geq\tens{\varpi}:\tens{C}_0:\tens{\varpi}\) for all \(\tens{\varpi}\). For isotropic heterogeneous (resp. reference) materials with bulk modulus \(\kappa\) (resp. \(\kappa_0\)) and shear modulus \(\mu\) (resp. \(\mu_0\)), this is equivalent to

\begin{equation*} \kappa\geq\kappa_0\quad\text{and}\quad\mu\geq\mu_0. \end{equation*}

Note that it is not always possible to find a reference material which complies with the requirements of the above theorem. A typical example would be a heterogeneous material conaining both pores [\(\tens{C}(\vec x) = \tens 0\)] and rigid inclusions [\(\tens{C}(\vec x)\rightarrow+\infty\)]. We won't address this issue for the time being.

Extremum principles are very useful in practice, as they usually lead to well-behaved numerical schemes. The energy principle of Hashin and Shtrikman also has deep theoretical consequences, as will be seen in the next instalment of this series.
