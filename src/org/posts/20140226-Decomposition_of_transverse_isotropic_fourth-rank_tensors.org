#+TITLE: Decomposition of transverse isotropic, fourth-rank tensors
#+DATE: <2014-02-26 Wed>

#+BEGIN_ABSTRACT
In [[file:./20140112-Elastic_constants_of_an_isotropic_material-03.org][a previous post]], I introduced the fourth-rank spherical and deviatoric projection tensors. Any isotropic fourth-rank tensor is a linear combination of these two tensors; in other words, the space of isotropic fourth-rank tensors (with minor and major symmetries) is of dimension 2. Similarly, it can be shown ([[file:../pages/references.org::#WALP1984][Walpole, 1984]]) that the space of three-dimensional, /transverse isotropic/ fourth-rank tensors (with minor and major symmetries) is of dimension 6. Furthermore; it is possible to produce a convenient basis of this space. This is the topic of the present post, which is mostly based on the paper by Walpole ([[file:../pages/references.org::#WALP1984][1984]]).
#+END_ABSTRACT

* Two second-rank projection tensors

It is recalled that transverse isotropy is defined by one single preferred direction. In the present post, $\vec n$ denotes the unit vector which indicates this preferred direction. Then, $\tensii p$ and $\tensii q$ are the following projectors

\begin{equation}
\newcommand{\p}{\tensii p}
\newcommand{\q}{\tensii q}
\label{eq:1}
\p=\vec n\otimes\vec n,\quad\q=\identii-\p.
\end{equation}

$\p$ is the projector onto the direction of anisotropy $\vec n$, while $\q$ is the projection onto the plane of isotropy (orthogonal to $\vec n$). It can readily be verified that

\begin{align*}
\p\cdot\p&=\p,&\q\cdot\q&=\q,&\p\cdot\q&=\q\cdot\p=\tensii 0,\\
\p:\p&=1,&\q:\q&=2,&\p:\q&=\q:\p=0.
\end{align*}

* Tensor product of linear transformations

It will be convenient to introduce a new, Kronecker-like product of two second-rank tensors, which Del Piero ([[file:../pages/references.org::#PIER1979][1979]]) named /tensor product of linear transformations/, and which will be denoted $\boxtimes{}$.

As a preliminary note, it should be observed that any fourth-rank tensor $\tensiv A$ can be viewed as a endomorphism over the space of second-rank tensors, since $\tensii x\mapsto\tensiv A:\tensii x$ is a linear mapping (the convention adopted in this blog for the double dot product has been specified in a [[file:./20140219-On_the_double_dot_product.org][previous post]]). Therefore, the fourth-rank tensor $\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b$ ($\tensii a, \tensii b$: second-rank tensors) can be defined by how it operates on second-rank tensors $\tensii x$

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\tensii x=\tensii a\cdot\tensii x\cdot\transp{\tensii b}.
\end{equation}

Writing the above definition in terms of the components of $\tensii a$, $\tensii b$ and $\tensii x$, it is found that $\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\tensii x=a_{ik}x_{kl}b_{jl}\vec e_i\otimes\vec e_j$, which shows that the components of $\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b$ are

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right)_{ijkl}=a_{ik}b_{jl},
\end{equation}

from which it results

\begin{equation*}
\transp{\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right)}=\transp{\tensii a}\boxtimes\transp{\tensii b}.
\end{equation*}

Given four second-rank tensors $\tensii a$, $\tensii b$, $\tensii c$ and $\tensii d$, the following identity holds

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:4}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\left(\tensii c\boxtimes\tensii d\right)=\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii c\right)\boxtimes\left(\tensii b\cdot\tensii d\right).
\end{equation}

Indeed, let $\tensii x$ be a second-rank tensor. Then, from Eq. \eqref{eq:2}

\begin{align*}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\left(\tensii c\boxtimes\tensii d\right):\tensii x&=\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\left(\tensii c\cdot\tensii x\cdot\transp{\tensii d}\right)=\tensii a\cdot\left(\tensii c\cdot\tensii x\cdot\transp{\tensii d}\right)\cdot\transp{\tensii b}\\
&=\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii c\right)\cdot\tensii x\cdot\left(\transp{\tensii d}\cdot\transp{\tensii b}\right)=\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii c\right)\cdot\tensii x\cdot\transp{\left(\tensii b\cdot\tensii d\right)}\\
&=\left(\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii c\right)\boxtimes\left(\tensii b\cdot\tensii d\right)\right):\tensii x.
\end{align*}

Eq. \eqref{eq:3} shows that even if $\tensii a$ and $\tensii b$ are symmetric, the tensor product $\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b$ does not necessarily have the minor symmetries. It will therefore be convenient to define $\tensii a\boxtimessym\tensii b$, as the tensor $\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b$, /symmetrized with respect to the last two indices/

\begin{equation*}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimessym\tensii b\right)_{ijkl}=\frac12\left(a_{ik}b_{jl}+a_{il}b_{jk}\right),
\end{equation*}

or, in intrinsic form ($\tensii x$: second-rank tensor)

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:5}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimessym\tensii b\right):\tensii x=\frac12\left(\tensii a\boxtimes\tensii b\right):\left(\tensii x+\transp{\tensii x}\right)=\frac12\tensii a\cdot\left(\tensii x+\transp{\tensii x}\right)\cdot\transp{\tensii b}.
\end{equation}

An identity similar to Eq. \eqref{eq:4} can then be derived with $\boxtimessym$

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:6}
\left(\tensii a\boxtimessym\tensii b\right):\left(\tensii c\boxtimessym\tensii d\right)=\frac12\left(\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii c\right)\boxtimessym\left(\tensii b\cdot\tensii d\right)+\left(\tensii a\cdot\tensii d\right)\boxtimessym\left(\tensii b\cdot\tensii c\right)\right).
\end{equation}

The following identities, involving the second-rank projectors $\p$ and $\q$ [see Eq. \eqref{eq:1}], readily follow from Eq. \eqref{eq:3}
\begin{align*}
\left(\p\boxtimessym\p\right):\p&=\p, & \left(\p\boxtimessym\p\right):\q&=\tensii 0,\\
\left(\q\boxtimessym\q\right):\p&=\tensii 0, & \left(\q\boxtimessym\q\right):\q&=\q,
\end{align*}
and
\begin{equation*}
\left(\p\boxtimessym\q\right):\p=\left(\p\boxtimessym\q\right):\q=\left(\q\boxtimessym\p\right):\p=\left(\q\boxtimessym\p\right):\q=\tensii 0.
\end{equation*}

To conclude, it is observed that the fourth-rank tensor $\identiv=\identii\boxtimessym\identii$ maps any second-rank tensor to its symmetric part: it reduces to the identity tensor over the space of second-rank, symmetric tensors.

* Walpole's basis for transverse isotropic tensors

Walpole ([[file:../pages/references.org::#WALP1984][1984]]) shows that any transverse isotropic tensor is a linear combination of the six fourth-rank tensors $\tensiv E_1,\tensiv E_2,\tensiv E_3,\tensiv E_4,\tensiv F,\tensiv G$ defined as follows

\begin{gather*}
\tensiv E_1=\p\otimes\p,\quad\tensiv E_2=\frac12\q\otimes\q,\quad\tensiv E_3=\frac1{\sqrt2}\p\otimes\q,\quad\tensiv E_4=\frac1{\sqrt2}\q\otimes\p\\
\tensiv F=\q\boxtimessym\q-\frac12\q\otimes\q,\quad\tensiv G=\p\boxtimessym\q+\q\boxtimessym\p.
\end{gather*}

* A convenient representation of transverse isotropic, fourth-rank tensors

Walpole ([[file:../pages/references.org::#WALP1984][1984]]) proposes a convenient representation of any transverse isotropic tensor $\tensiv T$, as the triplet $(A, f, g)$ where $A$ is a 2 Ã— 2 matrix and $f$ and $g$ are two scalars. This representation should be understood as

\begin{equation*}
\tensiv T=a_{11}\tensiv E_1+a_{22}\tensiv E_2+a_{12}\tensiv E_3+a_{21}\tensiv E_4+f\tensiv F+g\tensiv G,
\end{equation*}

where $a_{ij}$ are the coefficients of $A$. The condensed notation $\tensiv T=(A, f, g)$ shall be adopted. Using the multiplication table [[tab:2]], it can readily be verified that if $\tensiv T=(A, f, g)$ and $\tensiv T'=(A', f', g')$, then

\begin{equation*}
\tensiv T:\tensiv T'=(A\cdot A', ff', gg').
\end{equation*}

This representation is particularly convenient when it comes to inverting transverse isotropic tensors. Indeed,

\begin{equation*}
\tensiv T^{-1}=(A^{-1}, 1/f, 1/g).
\end{equation*}

Transposition is also straightforward

\begin{equation*}
\transp{\tensiv T}=(\transp{A}, f, g).
\end{equation*}

* Decomposition of fourth-rank, isotropic tensors in Walpole's basis

The isotropic tensors $\identiv$, $\sphprojiv$ and $\devprojiv$ introduced [[file:./20140112-Elastic_constants_of_an_isotropic_material-03.org][previously]] can be considered as transverse isotropic tensors. As such, they can be decomposed in Walpole's basis. First, the identity tensor $\identiv$ decomposes as follows

\begin{align*}
\identiv&=\identii\boxtimessym\identii=\left(\p+\q\right)\boxtimessym\left(\p+\q\right)\\
&=\p\boxtimessym\p+\p\boxtimessym\q+\q\boxtimessym\p+\q\boxtimessym\q\\
&=\tensiv E_1+\tensiv G+\tensiv F+\tensiv E_2.
\end{align*}

where the identity $\p\boxtimessym\p=\p\otimes\p=\tensiv E_1$ has been used. Now, the decomposition of $\sphprojiv$ is readily derived from its definition

\begin{align*}
3\sphprojiv&=\identii\otimes\identii=\left(\p+\q\right)\otimes\left(\p+\q\right)\\
&=\p\otimes\p+\p\otimes\q+\q\otimes\p+\q\otimes\q\\
&=\tensiv E_1+\sqrt2\tensiv E_3+\sqrt2\tensiv E_4+2\tensiv E_2.
\end{align*}

Finally, the decomposition of the deviatoric projection tensor $\devprojiv$ is found from the definition $\devprojiv=\identiv-\sphprojiv$. To sum up

\begin{gather*}
\identiv=\left(\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}, 1, 1\right),\\
\sphprojiv=\left(\frac13\begin{bmatrix}1&\sqrt2\\\sqrt2&2\end{bmatrix}, 0, 0\right),\\
\devprojiv=\left(\frac13\begin{bmatrix}2&-\sqrt2\\-\sqrt2&1\end{bmatrix}, 1, 1\right).
\end{gather*}

* Conclusion

In the present post, a convenient decomposition of three-dimensional, fourth-rank, transverse isotropic tensors as a linear combination of six basis tensors has been proposed. Multiplication (in the sense of the [[file:./20140219-On_the_double_dot_product.org][double dot product]]) of these basis tensors is straightforward (see Table [[tab:2]]). Besides, representing transverse isotropic tensors as a (matrix, scalar, scalar) triplet eases their multiplication and inversion. Walpole's basis turns out to be very handy in many situations (including the derivation of the Green operator for strains).
